# Steelman Policy

## Principle

A counterhypothesis must appear in its strongest plausible form.

Weak reconstruction of a counterhypothesis is a form of bias.

## What steelmanning is not

The following are not steelman reconstructions:

- "This hypothesis is not proven." *(dismissal dressed as analysis)*
- "Some believe X, but most experts disagree." *(appeal to majority)*
- "This would require coordination that seems unlikely." *(assertion without evidence)*

## What a steelman requires

For each counterhypothesis subject to steelman review, the record must include:

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| `strongest_version` | The hypothesis stated in its most coherent and evidence-connected form |
| `best_supporting_evidence` | The strongest evidence items that support this hypothesis |
| `strongest_objection_to_mainline` | The strongest challenge this hypothesis poses to the primary interpretation |
| `what_would_change_the_verdict` | What evidence or argument, if confirmed, would force a revision |
| `quality_score` | Self-assessed quality of the steelman reconstruction (0.0–1.0) |

## Hard rule

> No final verdict if the strongest counterhypothesis was not steelmanned.

A `quality_score` below 0.5 on the strongest counterhypothesis blocks a final assessment and triggers a required revision.

## Why this matters

Political assessments routinely fail not because they ignore counterhypotheses entirely, but because they reconstruct them weakly and then dismiss the weak version. This is sometimes called a straw man. The steelman obligation inverts this: the counterhypothesis must be made as strong as possible before it is weighed against the primary interpretation.

If the primary interpretation cannot survive a well-constructed counterhypothesis, that is information, not a problem to be hidden.
